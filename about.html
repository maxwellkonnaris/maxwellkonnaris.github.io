About Me

I’m a scientist and problem solver with hands-on experience spanning the full spectrum of biomedical research—from the clinic to the lab bench to computational modeling. My training began in applied physiology, where I learned to translate clinical insights into research questions. In wet-lab settings, I focused on regenerative medicine, biomarker identification, and drug design, while also contributing to clinical trial work for therapeutics and medical devices. As a PhD candidate in the Silverman and Lazar Labs at Penn State, I honed my skills in genomic analysis, algorithmic development for genomic barcoding and biomarker discovery, and high-dimensional -omic statistical modeling. Through it all, I’ve strived to bridge theory and practice—leveraging rigorous method development in statistics and bioinformatics to turn raw data into actionable insights that drive meaningful biomedical innovation using rigorous and reproducible approaches. For a more specific understanding of my professional background and accomplishments, check out my CV.

Biomedical Experience

Research Focus

Rigor and Reproducibility of Network Inference Methods


Networks are foundational across biomedical innovation. Constructed networks and network graphs are used to connect functional pathways, disease states, integrate multimodal data, identify biomarkers, and design drug targets. My work explores the reproducibility and statistical rigor of methods that infer these networks from sequencing and non-graph data sources.

Machine Learning and AI Statistical Rigor


Advances in machine learning and artificial intelligence offer unprecedented opportunities for data-driven discovery. My research focuses on the theoretical underpinnings and statistical rigor of these methods, ensuring robust and interpretable results for biomedical applications.

Drug Design, Biomarker Identification, and Diagnostics


Genomic sequences can be mined to determine unique barcodes that identify particular taxonomies. These sequences not only serve as diagnostic indicators for strain detection but also reveal critical insights into functional and disease pathways. My research aims to bridge computational genomics with actionable diagnostics and therapeutic strategies.

Outreach

Engineering Education for Underrepresented Communities

I collaborated with E.L.i.T.E. in Harlem, NYC, and Global Code in Accra, Ghana to develop curriculum, structure learning, assist in hands-on material, and teach classroom lectures. Engineering material was tailored to different education levels:

  • Beginner: Integrated engineering principles with math, physics, and applied technology, teaching students to translate coursework into real-world applications.
  • Advanced: Created applications solving real-world problems using Raspberry Pis/Arduinos with circuitry, robotics, and larger engineered prototypes.

Mentorship for Aspiring STEM Professionals

As a volunteer mentor, I guided middle school, high school, and undergraduate students through their professional development. I believe in sharing lessons learned to help others seeking advice or pursuing their interests in STEM. I participated in programs such as:

Genomix Graduate Student Leadership

As the president and board member of Genomix, a student-run organization at Penn State University, I represented the bioinformatics and genomics community. Key contributions include:

  • Growing the club from 50 to 180 members through content generation, public presentations, and social gatherings.
  • Developing graduate school guides/resources to reduce stress across students' timelines.
