About Me

Background & Interests

I am a PhD candidate in Bioinformatics and Genomics at Pennsylvania State University, where I am advised by two statisticans: Prof. Justin Silverman, MD, PhD and Prof. Nicole Lazar, PhD.

I am interested in identifying and solving broad problems at scale. I am motivated by the potential of technology to accelerate scientific discovery and improve human health. My work focuses on a major gap in the rigor of scientific methods for analyzing high-throughput sequencing data. I aim to help close that gap by studying the information content of data, evaluating limitations, and developing robust/practical analytical frameworks to solve these challenges.

Without rigor, flawed methodology can produce findings that do not replicate, waste resources, erode trust in science, and slow the pace of discovery.

I believe scientific modeling requires a deep understanding of both the underlying translational context and the statistical/computational principles involved. I possess a unique combination of the statistical depth, translational understanding, and computational skills essential for building methods that are not only theoretically sound, but also practical for real-world problems guided by a science/medical background.

Teaching & Mentoring Preface
Preface
About This Book

This book grew out of years of teaching graduate-level methods courses, supervising undergraduate theses, and mentoring early-career researchers. The chapters that follow are not a comprehensive treatise — they are dispatches from the field, written close to the problems that students actually encounter.

Each chapter addresses one recurring challenge: how to make research reproducible, how to reason probabilistically about uncertain data, how to build workflows that survive collaboration and revision, and finally, how to collect the small lessons that don't fit anywhere else.

The material is unapologetically practical. Where theory appears, it is there to illuminate practice, not to demonstrate mastery of it. Where examples appear, they are drawn from real situations with the identifying details removed.

· · ·

My hope is that a student midway through a stuck project might open this, read one chapter, and feel less alone. The problems described here are common. The solutions are learnable. The failures are survivable.

Chapter I Reproducibility
Chapter One
Reproducibility

Reproducibility is not a bureaucratic virtue. It is the mechanism by which science corrects itself, and the single most powerful gift a researcher can give to their community and to their future self. A result that cannot be reproduced is, at best, anecdote.

The reproducibility crisis that swept through psychology, medicine, and the social sciences was not a scandal about dishonesty. It was a scandal about defaults — defaults in workflow, in statistical practice, in how researchers managed and reported their work. Changing those defaults is not difficult. It requires habit, not heroism.

What Reproducibility Actually Means

There is a spectrum. At one end: replicability — running the same experiment again and obtaining similar results. At the other: robustness — arriving at the same conclusion via a different approach entirely. In between sits reproducibility proper: given your data and your code, another researcher can re-run your analysis and obtain identical numerical results.

Computational reproducibility is the easiest form to achieve and the most commonly neglected. It requires only three things: version-controlled code, documented data provenance, and an explicit software environment.

Chapter I Reproducibility
Version Control Is Non-Negotiable

Every research project should live in a version-controlled repository from day one. Not from the day you think it's worth sharing — from day one. Git is the standard tool. The learning curve is real but not steep; most researchers need only five commands to work effectively.

Version control serves two purposes simultaneously: it creates an audit trail of your analytical decisions, and it enables collaboration without catastrophe. Both are undervalued until the moment they are desperately needed.

Practical rule: Commit after every meaningful change. Write commit messages as if explaining your reasoning to a future collaborator who has no context — because that future collaborator is you, six months from now.

Documenting Data Provenance

Raw data should be treated as read-only. Never overwrite it. Every transformation — cleaning, merging, filtering — should be performed by a script that reads raw data and produces a derived dataset. This pipeline should be runnable from scratch, without any manual steps.

If your data came from an external source, record the exact URL, the access date, and the version or edition. Data changes. URLs break. The record you keep today may be the only record that will ever exist.

Chapter II Probabilistic Modeling
Chapter Two
Probabilistic Modeling

The world does not present itself to researchers in certainties. Data arrives with noise, models are simplifications of reality, and conclusions must always be hedged. Learning to think probabilistically — to reason fluently about uncertainty — is perhaps the most transferable intellectual skill a quantitative education can cultivate.

And yet it is routinely neglected. Students learn to run regressions before they understand what a probability distribution is. They learn to report p-values before they can say what a p-value means. This ordering does lasting harm.

Starting with Priors

The Bayesian framework offers a coherent language for scientific reasoning. Its central contribution is not the formula — Bayes' theorem is elementary probability — but the vocabulary. Prior beliefs, updated by evidence, yield posterior beliefs. This is not a statistical technique. It is a description of how learning works.

Prior distributions are controversial among researchers trained in the frequentist tradition. The objection is that priors are "subjective." This is true, and it is not a problem. Subjectivity made explicit and documented is epistemically superior to subjectivity hidden in model choices and stopping rules.

Chapter II Probabilistic Modeling
Communicating Uncertainty

Researchers who think probabilistically still often communicate as if certainty were attainable. This is partly habit, partly pressure from editors and reviewers who prefer clean conclusions, and partly genuine confusion about the relationship between statistical and epistemic uncertainty.

Uncertainty should be communicated in the natural units of the phenomenon. A 95% credible interval for effect size, expressed in the units of the outcome variable, conveys more than a standardized coefficient and a p-value ever can.

Common Modeling Pitfalls

Overfitting remains the most common modeling failure. A model that describes its training data perfectly has not learned about the world — it has memorized noise. Cross-validation is the appropriate corrective.

Ignoring the measurement model is nearly as common. Every variable in your dataset was measured by some process carrying its own uncertainty. Composite indices, self-report measures, and administrative records each carry characteristic error structures that should be modeled explicitly, not assumed away.

Confusing absence of evidence with evidence of absence is a logical error that probabilistic training helps prevent. A non-significant result is a statement about power, not about the null hypothesis.

Chapter III Practical Workflows
Chapter Three
Practical Workflows

Workflow is where good intentions meet the friction of actual research. The researcher who understands reproducibility and probabilistic reasoning still needs a practical system for moving from raw data to finished analysis — one that survives collaboration, revision, interruption, and the passage of time.

The goal is not elegance for its own sake. The goal is a project that another person — or your future self — can pick up and understand without a guided tour. That standard is higher than most researchers set for themselves. It is also lower than many fear.

Project Structure

A consistent project directory structure pays dividends immediately. Raw data goes in data/raw/, never to be touched. Derived data goes in data/processed/, always reproducible from the scripts that created it. Analysis scripts are numbered in execution order: 01_clean.R, 02_merge.R, 03_model.R.

A Makefile or equivalent build file can run them all in sequence. A README at the project root explains what the project is, where the data came from, and how to reproduce the analysis end-to-end.

5
Chapter III Practical Workflows
Collaboration Without Catastrophe

Collaborative research introduces synchronization problems that solo research avoids. Two researchers editing the same file simultaneously, in different versions of the same software, is a situation that has ended more than one collaboration. Version control solves the synchronization problem. Containerization — using Docker or a package manager like renv or conda — solves the software environment problem.

On code review: Every analysis benefits from a second pair of eyes before results are reported externally. This is not a judgment on competence. It is a structural safeguard against the errors that authors are constitutionally unable to see in their own work.

The Iteration Cycle

Research rarely proceeds linearly. Exploratory analysis generates hypotheses; hypotheses drive confirmatory analysis; confirmatory results reveal new questions that demand further exploration. A good workflow accommodates this cycling without conflating the two phases.

The critical discipline is separating exploratory notebooks — where ideas are developed freely and messily — from confirmatory scripts, which implement pre-specified analyses and should not be modified after the first look at the outcome data. Mixing them is the most common source of unintentional researcher degrees of freedom.

Chapter IV Notes & Reflections
Chapter Four
Notes & Reflections

This chapter collects the things that didn't fit anywhere else. Some are observations about the practice of research. Some are advice I give so often in office hours that writing it down seemed more efficient. Some are things I wish someone had told me earlier.

On Failure

Most experiments fail. Most models don't fit. Most hypotheses are wrong. This is not a problem with the researcher; it is a feature of inquiry. The researcher's job is not to generate correct results on the first attempt — it is to update beliefs appropriately and iterate efficiently.

The most dangerous mindset in research is the belief that failure is informative only when it produces a publishable null result. Failures that redirect attention, refine hypotheses, or reveal measurement problems are doing exactly what failures are supposed to do.

On Writing

Writing is analysis. The act of explaining a result in plain language forces a clarity of thought that no amount of modeling can substitute for. If you cannot say what your finding means without jargon, you do not yet understand it well enough to publish it.

Write early and continuously. Do not wait until the analysis is finished. A methods section written while the methods are being implemented is more accurate, and less painful to produce, than one reconstructed afterward.

Chapter IV Notes & Reflections
On Mentoring

The best mentoring I have received shared one characteristic: it treated my problems as interesting. Not as evidence of deficiency, not as obstacles to be removed, but as genuinely interesting puzzles worth thinking through together. That quality — curiosity about someone else's problem — is both rare and learnable.

A mentee who brings a problem they've already partially solved is doing you the favor of thinking before they speak. Reward that. Ask them to explain their partial solution before offering your own.

Recommended Reading

Three works stand out for their practical utility: Gandrud's Reproducible Research with R and RStudio, Gelman and Hill's Data Analysis Using Regression and Multilevel Models, and Wilson et al.'s "Good Enough Practices in Scientific Computing" (PLOS Computational Biology, 2017). All three reward re-reading at different career stages.
